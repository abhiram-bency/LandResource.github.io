Land resources

Land resources refer to the natural resources that are found on or in the land. These resources include soil, water, vegetation, minerals, and wildlife. They are essential for human survival and economic development. Soil is important for agriculture, providing a medium for plant growth and supplying nutrients to plants. Water is crucial for drinking, irrigation, and industrial purposes. Vegetation, including forests and grasslands, provides habitat for wildlife, helps maintain biodiversity, and offers resources such as timber and food. Minerals are mined from the earth and used in various industries, while wildlife is important for ecological balance and tourism. Efficient and sustainable management of land resources is essential to ensure their availability for future generations.

Classifiaction of land resources in India

Land resources can be classified in several ways, depending on the criteria used. Here are some common classifications:

Based on Land Use:

Agricultural Land: Land used for farming, including crop cultivation, animal husbandry, and agroforestry.

Forest Land: Land covered by forests, including natural forests, plantations, and woodlands.

Urban Land: Land used for residential, commercial, industrial, and infrastructural purposes in urban areas.

Rural Land: Land used for non-urban purposes, often including agricultural, forested, and natural areas.

Based on Ownership:

Public Land: Land owned by the government and managed for public use, such as parks, forests, and roads.

Private Land: Land owned by individuals, corporations, or organizations for private use, such as residential or commercial purposes.

Based on Productivity:

Cultivable Land: Land suitable for cultivation, often with fertile soil and adequate water supply.

Non-Cultivable Land: Land unsuitable for cultivation, such as deserts, mountains, and urban areas. Barren and wasteland for example, rocky, arid, deserts, etc covers 8.61% of land. Moreover, laud for non agriculture uses like building, roads, factories and settlements. etc takes upto 5.57%. Also, the, other uncultivated lands mainly consist of Permanent pastures and grazing land which takes up 3.38%; Land under miscellaneous tree crops groves which is 1.11% and culturable waste land which is 4.17%

Fallow land: Total fallow land in land resources contains 8.13% out of which 4,76% covers the curren fallow. Current fallow in land not cultivated from less than one agricultural year. Lands left uncultivated for one to five years contains 3.37%. If fallow lands are cultivated once or twice in 2 to 3 years, and if these are included in NSA then the percentage of NSA in India will be around 54% for that particular area.

Net Sown Area (NSA): Area sown more than once in an agricultural year in addition to net sowa or gross cropped area is called net sown area or NSA. Net sown area is available in 46.24% in total land resources in India. States of India like Punjab and Haryana have 80% of net sown area from total area. Whereas, Arunachal Pradesh, Mizoram, Manipur and Andaman Nicobar Islands have less than 10% of net sown area from total area

Based on Natural Features:

Arable Land: Land suitable for growing crops.

Pasture Land: Land used for grazing livestock

Wetland: Land characterized by wet conditions, such as marshes, swamps, and bogs.

Based on Ecological Function:

Natural Land: Land with intact ecosystems and biodiversity, often undisturbed by human activities.

Degraded Land: Land that has been degraded due to human activities, such as deforestation, pollution, or overgrazing.

Based on Legal Status:

Reserved Land: Land set aside for specific purposes, such as conservation, recreation, or military use.

Allocated Land: Land assigned for specific uses, such as agriculture, industry, or residential development.
